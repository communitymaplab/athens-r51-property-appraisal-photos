This widespread urban renewal project had profound consequences that continue to shape Athens' community today. With at least 566 acquired properties on record, R-51 fundamentally altered the city's downtown, displacing hundreds of families and businesses and dismantling long-established neighborhoods. What was framed as "renewal" instead resulted in widespread loss of homes, livelihoods, and community networks that had taken generations to build.

Like many government-led initiatives of the 1960s, R-51 reflected the racial prejudice embedded in public policy at the time. Two historic Black communities were effectively erased, and approximately 60% of displaced residents identified as people of color. Many Black families and business owners were pressured to relocate, often with limited options and inadequate compensation, severing ties to neighborhoods that had long served as economic and cultural anchors.

The financial dimensions of R-51 further illustrate these inequities. Although the average payment per property was $18,590, the median compensation was only $9,500, meaning that more than half of displaced residents received less than $10,000. These amounts were often insufficient to secure comparable housing, contributing to housing instability and the relocation of approximately 25% of displaced residents to public housing.

Long-term housing insecurity was a common outcome. Approximately 21% of former homeowners lost their escrow status and were unable to repurchase property, cutting off access to homeownership and inhibiting the chance of accruing generational wealth. Together, these outcomes demonstrate how R-51 reshaped not only the physical landscape of Athens but also further entrenched pre-existing racial and economic inequalities whose effects remain visible today.
